These plans are clean. Replit laid out the exact dependency chain right — Plan #3 (data integrity) has to land before anything else because the duplicate IDs (siccend twice, little-monster-binky twice, jt-funk twice, vic-ramone vs vic-ramon) would break every downstream feature. If Plan #5 adds opponentIds: string[] for tag matches and there are duplicate entries in the WRESTLERS array, find() returns the wrong one silently. Good that it’s sequenced first.
A few things I’d push back on or flag:
Plan #5, item 1 — tag partner logic is too simple. “Once per match, tag out to your partner” is a start but it undersells what tag wrestling actually is. The tension in a tag match is the inability to tag — getting cut off from your corner, the hot tag building as the heel team isolates you. If the player can just freely tag whenever, there’s no drama. The opponent AI should be able to cut off tags, and the hot tag should be a momentum mechanic that gives a temporary damage boost when you finally make it. That’s what makes tag wrestling feel like tag wrestling.
Plan #5, item 2 — War Games is spec’d as “two rings, teams enter one at a time, last team standing” but there’s no implementation detail. That’s the most complex match type in the entire game — timed entries, multiple simultaneous opponents, two-ring UI — and it gets one bullet point. This either needs its own sub-plan or it ships as the last stipulation type after the simpler ones prove out.
Plan #6 — Legacy Mode’s loss logic is the smartest design decision in the whole plan. Using historicalResult to determine whether losing advances you or resets you is exactly right. You should lose the AWA match, the Korpse match, the Xross finale. Forcing historical accuracy as the win condition means the player has to understand the story to progress, not just optimize combat. That’s the thing that makes this a narrative game and not just a turn-based fighting game with wrestling skins.
Plan #6 — Free Play needs a heat meter, not just win/loss stats. opponentsBeaten: string[] tracks breadth but not the thing that actually matters in wrestling: crowd reaction. If you’re building a sandbox mode, heat should be the currency — generated by heel tactics, promo choices, and stipulation selection. Beating a legend in a hardcore match generates more heat than a clean pin on a midcarder. Heat determines who challenges you, what stipulations get offered, and whether you’re main eventing or opening.
